# Agriculture Information Portal - Q&A Dataset

## 🌾 Rice
**1. Farmer Question:** The leaves of my rice plants are turning yellow with brown spots, starting from the tips. What disease is this and how can I stop it?
**Expert Answer:** This sounds like Brown Spot disease, often triggered by poor soil health and nutrient deficiency. I recommend applying a balanced NPK fertilizer. For immediate control, use a foliar spray of Propiconazole 25% EC at 1 ml per liter of water. Ensure proper water management in your paddy.

**2. Farmer Question:** My rice field is heavily infested with stem borers causing "dead hearts". What is the best pesticide for this?
**Expert Answer:** Stem borers are a common threat. Apply Cartap Hydrochloride 4G at 10 kg per acre in standing water. Alternatively, you can spray Chlorantraniliprole 18.5% SC at 60 ml per acre. Also, consider setting up pheromone traps (3-4 per acre) to monitor and trap adult moths.

**3. Farmer Question:** How much water is actually needed for the vegetative stage of rice? Should the field always be flooded?
**Expert Answer:** While rice is a water-loving crop, continuous flooding is not required and wastes water. During the vegetative stage, maintain just 2-3 cm of standing water. Practicing Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD) can save up to 30% of water, improve root growth, and reduce methane emissions without affecting yield.

**4. Farmer Question:** I notice a white, powdery fungal growth on the base of the rice stems near the water level. What should I do?
**Expert Answer:** This is Sheath Blight, a dangerous fungal infection that spreads in high humidity. Drain the excess water from the field for a few days immediately. Spray Hexaconazole 5% EC at 2 ml per liter of water or Validamycin 3% L at 2.5 ml per liter, ensuring the spray reaches the base of the plants.

**5. Farmer Question:** When is the best time to apply the top dressing of urea to my rice crop?
**Expert Answer:** Urea should ideally be applied in three split doses to minimize nitrogen loss. Apply 25% as basal during transplanting, 50% during the active tillering stage (around 20-25 days after transplanting), and the final 25% at the panicle initiation stage (around 45-50 days after transplanting).

## 🌾 Wheat
**6. Farmer Question:** There are yellow stripes of powder appearing on the leaves of my wheat crop. It rubs off on my fingers. How do I treat it?
**Expert Answer:** You are dealing with Yellow Rust, a highly destructive fungal disease. Immediately spray Tebuconazole 25% WG at 1 gram per liter or Propiconazole 25% EC at 1 ml per liter of water. Ensure thorough coverage of the foliage and apply a second spray after 15 days if the weather remains cool and humid.

**7. Farmer Question:** The wheat grains seem shriveled and small this year due to an early heatwave. Is there anything I can spray to improve grain filling?
**Expert Answer:** Terminal heat stress severely impacts grain weight. To mitigate this, spray a 2% Potassium Nitrate (KNO3) solution during the anthesis (flowering) and early grain-filling stages. It helps regulate stomata, delays leaf senescence, and improves grain plumpness under heat stress.

**8. Farmer Question:** I have a severe infestation of broad-leaf weeds in my wheat field. It's been 30 days since sowing. What herbicide is safe?
**Expert Answer:** At 30-35 days after sowing, you can safely apply Metsulfuron Methyl 20% WP at 8 grams per acre, or 2,4-D Ethyl Ester at 250 ml per acre mixed with 150 liters of water. Ensure you spray when there is sufficient moisture in the soil for maximum effectiveness.

**9. Farmer Question:** How many critical irrigations does the wheat crop need if water is limited?
**Expert Answer:** If water is limited, the most critical stage for irrigation is the Crown Root Initiation (CRI) stage, which occurs 20-25 days after sowing. The second most important is the booting/flowering stage (80-85 days). Never skip irrigation at these two stages to avoid massive yield losses.

**10. Farmer Question:** My wheat seedlings are turning pale green to yellow, and the older leaves are dying off at the tips.
**Expert Answer:** This is a classic symptom of Nitrogen deficiency, common in light soils or if earlier rains caused leaching. Apply a light top-dressing of Urea (about 15-20 kg per acre) just before your next scheduled irrigation, or apply a 2% urea foliar spray for quicker absorption.

## 🥔 Potato
**11. Farmer Question:** My potato plants look healthy, but the tubers have deep, rough, scabby lesions on the skin. Why does this happen?
**Expert Answer:** This is Common Scab, a bacterial disease caused by Streptomyces scabies. It thrives in dry, alkaline soils (pH above 6.0). Avoid using fresh farmyard manure. Next season, use certified disease-free seed potatoes, maintain consistent soil moisture during tuber initiation, and consider crop rotation with legumes.

**12. Farmer Question:** The leaves of my potato plants are showing large, irregular dark brown lesions with a pale green halo, and there's a white mold underneath in the morning.
**Expert Answer:** This is Late Blight, a devastating disease capable of destroying the crop within days. Immediately spray a systemic fungicide like Metalaxyl 8% + Mancozeb 64% WP at 2.5 grams per liter of water. Repeat after 7-10 days if cloudy, humid weather persists.

**13. Farmer Question:** How do I increase the size and weight of my potato tubers before harvesting?
**Expert Answer:** Tuber bulking requires high Potassium. Apply Sulfate of Potash (SOP) via drip fertigation or as a foliar spray (00:00:50) at 5 grams per liter about 30-40 days before the expected harvest date. Ensure the soil is moist but not waterlogged.

**14. Farmer Question:** When should I cut the vines before digging up the potatoes, and why is this necessary?
**Expert Answer:** Vine killing (dehaulming) should be done 10-15 days before harvesting. You can do this by cutting the stems manually or spraying a desiccant. This stops tuber growth, prevents late blight spores from washing down to the tubers, and forces the potato skins to harden, reducing harvest damage.

**15. Farmer Question:** I'm seeing small, green, soft-bodied insects clustering under the potato leaves. They are stunting plant growth.
**Expert Answer:** Those are aphids. They suck plant sap and transmit serious viral diseases like Potato Leafroll Virus. Spray Imidacloprid 17.8% SL at 0.5 ml per liter or Thiamethoxam 25% WG at 0.5 grams per liter. Ensure complete coverage of the undersides of the leaves.

## 🌽 Maize (Corn)
**16. Farmer Question:** Large holes are appearing in the central whorl of my young maize plants, and there is sawdust-like frass. What is the pest?
**Expert Answer:** This is damage from the Fall Armyworm (FAW), a severe pest for maize. Spray Spinetoram 11.7% SC at 0.5 ml per liter or Emamectin Benzoate 5% SG at 0.4 grams per liter. Direct the spray nozzle directly into the plant whorls where the caterpillars hide.

**17. Farmer Question:** The edges of my maize leaves are turning purple and reddish. The plants are also growing very slowly.
**Expert Answer:** Purple coloring on leaf margins in young maize is a distinct sign of Phosphorus deficiency, often triggered by cold soils that prevent root absorption. Once temperatures rise, it usually resolves, but you should apply a water-soluble foliar spray of NPK (19-19-19) to give the plants a quick boost.

**18. Farmer Question:** Only half the kernels on my maize cobs are developing; the tips are completely blank. How do I fix this?
**Expert Answer:** Blank ear tips usually indicate poor pollination caused by severe heat or drought stress during the silking stage. It can also be due to nitrogen or potassium deficiency later in the season. Ensure critical irrigation is delivered during the tasseling and silking stages.

**19. Farmer Question:** What is the ideal spacing for a high-yielding hybrid maize variety to prevent overcrowding?
**Expert Answer:** For optimal yields, maintain a row-to-row spacing of 60 cm (24 inches) and a plant-to-plant spacing of 20-25 cm (8-10 inches) within the row. This provides a plant population of roughly 26,000 to 30,000 plants per acre, ensuring adequate sunlight and nutrient uptake.

## 🍅 Tomato
**20. Farmer Question:** The bottom ends of my tomatoes are turning black, sunken, and leathery. It's ruining the fruit. How do I cure this?
**Expert Answer:** This is Blossom End Rot. It is not a disease, but a Calcium deficiency in the fruit, usually caused by irregular watering which prevents roots from taking up calcium. Maintain consistent soil moisture using drip irrigation and mulching. Apply a foliar spray of Calcium Nitrate at 2-3 grams per liter.

**21. Farmer Question:** The leaves on my tomato plants are curling upward and becoming thick and brittle, but they remain green.
**Expert Answer:** This is Tomato Leaf Curl Virus (ToLCV), which is transmitted by whiteflies. There is no cure for the virus once infected. Uproot and burn severely infected plants. To protect the remaining crop, aggressively manage whiteflies by placing yellow sticky traps and spraying Diafenthiuron 50% WP.

**22. Farmer Question:** Small, serpentine white tunnels are appearing all over the surface of my tomato leaves.
**Expert Answer:** This is the work of the Leafminer fly larvae, which eat the inner tissue of the leaves. Severe infestations reduce photosynthesis. Spray Abamectin 1.9% EC at 1 ml per liter or Neem oil (1500 ppm) at 3-5 ml per liter during early morning or late evening.

**23. Farmer Question:** What is the best fertilizer timeline for indeterminate tomato varieties grown on trellises?
**Expert Answer:** Indeterminate tomatoes produce fruit continuously. Provide a basal dose of Phosphorus and Potassium. Once blooming begins, fertigate weekly with a calcium-rich fertilizer. Switch to high Potassium (like 0-0-50) during heavy fruit set and ripening phases to ensure fruit size, color, and firmness.

## 🧅 Onion
**24. Farmer Question:** The tips of my onion leaves are dying back and turning brown, and I see purplish, oval spots lower down on the leaves.
**Expert Answer:** This is Purple Blotch, a major fungal disease of onions, worse in warm, wet weather. Spray Mancozeb 75% WP at 2.5g/liter mixed with a silicon-based spreader/sticker (since onion leaves are waxy). Repeat after 10-12 days if conditions remain humid.

**25. Farmer Question:** My harvested onions are rotting in storage from the neck down. The flesh becomes watery and smells bad. 
**Expert Answer:** This is Neck Rot. To prevent this, withhold irrigation 10-15 days before harvest. Do not harvest when the weather is wet. Cut the tops leaving 2-3 cm attached to the bulb, and ensure the bulbs are properly cured (dried in shade with good ventilation) until the necks are completely tight and dry before storage.

**26. Farmer Question:** I am seeing tiny, slender, yellowish insects congregating in the axils of the onion leaves. They leave silvery patches on the leaves.
**Expert Answer:** These are Onion Thrips. They scrape the leaf surface and suck the sap, weakening the plant. Because they hide deep in the leaf axils, you need a systemic insecticide. Spray Fipronil 5% SC at 1.5 ml per liter or Spinetoram 11.7% SC at 1 ml per liter, ensuring good coverage.

## 🌱 Soybean
**27. Farmer Question:** The leaves of my soybean crop have yellow mosaic-like patterns, and the plants look stunted.
**Expert Answer:** This is Yellow Mosaic Virus (YMV), transmitted by whiteflies. The virus cannot be cured. You must focus on vector control. Spray systemic insecticides like Thiamethoxam 25 WG at 100g/acre or Acetamiprid 20 SP to kill the whiteflies and stop the spread. Next year, plant YMV-resistant varieties.

**28. Farmer Question:** How do I ensure better nodule formation on my soybean roots for nitrogen fixation?
**Expert Answer:** To maximize root nodules, treat the soybean seeds with Rhizobium japonicum culture and Phosphorus Solubilizing Bacteria (PSB) before sowing. Also, ensure your soil is not deficient in Molybdenum and Cobalt, which are micro-nutrients essential for the nitrogen-fixing bacteria to function.

## 🎋 Sugarcane
**29. Farmer Question:** Deep red lesions with white cross-bands are appearing on the midribs of my sugarcane leaves, and the canes smell like alcohol when split.
**Expert Answer:** This is Red Rot, one of the most dreaded diseases of sugarcane. There is no chemical cure for an active field infection. You must uproot and burn the affected clumps immediately to stop the spread. Do not ratoon the infected crop. For the next planting, use healthy, certified seed setts treated with Carbendazim.

**30. Farmer Question:** The internodes of my sugarcane are getting bored into by caterpillars, and the growing shoot has died, turning brown. 
**Expert Answer:** This represents Early Shoot Borer damage. To manage this naturally, release Trichogramma chilonis egg parasitoids starting from 30 days after planting. For chemical control, apply Chlorantraniliprole 0.4% GR at 7.5 kg per acre in the soil at the time of planting or first earthing up.
